03 / The Problem

Like most students, I thought studying for 5-6 hours a day meant I was being productive. But my mock test scores were stuck around the 65-70 mark. I wanted to know if my long study hours were actually translating into better scores, or if I was simply burning myself out without making real progress.

04 / My Approach

Instead of guessing, I turned myself into a dataset. For 30 days, I logged every study session: the subject, the exact hours, and an honest self-rating of my Focus Level (High, Medium, Low). I then recorded my weekly mock test scores.

I loaded this custom dataset into Python using Pandas, cleaned the logs, and cross-tabulated focus levels against average mock scores to find the real drivers of academic performance.

06 / Key Data Insights
Insight 01 — Time is a Vanity Metric
Sessions with "High Focus" yielded an average score of 76.73, while "Low Focus" sessions dropped to 64.76. This ~12-point gap existed even when the total hours logged were exactly the same. Quality completely outweighs quantity.
Insight 02 — Burnout is Visible
The daily trend line showed clear volatility. Days with extreme hours (6+ hours) were almost always followed by a severe drop in study hours the next day, proving that sprinting leads to burnout, not consistency.
Insight 03 — Effort Doesn't Guarantee Score
Math took the most hours (38h) and gave the highest score (79 points). However, GK took 23 hours but yielded the lowest score (62). Simply spending more time on a weak subject didn't automatically fix the performance gap.

07 / The Outcome & Action Taken
I completely stopped obsessing over the 6-hour daily mark. I restructured my schedule around 2-hour "Deep Work" blocks with strict breaks. By treating my focus as a finite resource, I increased my average mock scores and eliminated burnout within just two weeks.
